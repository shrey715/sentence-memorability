# Conclusion

## Summary of Key Findings

This study investigated how noun imageability and grammatical voice affect sentence memorability in a continuous recognition paradigm. Three principal findings emerged from the preliminary analysis of 112 participants:

1. **Noun memorability has a threshold effect on sentence recognition.** Sentences with two low-memorability nouns (LL) were recognised significantly less well than sentences containing at least one high-memorability noun (HH, HL, LH). Critically, there was no difference between HH, HL, and LH conditions, suggesting that a single high-memorability noun is sufficient to anchor the sentence in memory. This pattern was confirmed by both corrected recognition scores (*H*(3) = 11.26, *p* = .010) and signal detection sensitivity *d'* (*H*(3) = 8.60, *p* = .035), with small but consistent pairwise effect sizes (Cliff's δ ≈ 0.16–0.20 for LL vs. other conditions).

2. **Grammatical voice does not affect sentence recognition or wording discrimination.** Neither active nor passive voice conferred a recognition advantage (IR: *p* = .374; WR: *p* = .113), and there was no noun condition × voice interaction (*p* = .975). This is consistent with theories proposing that sentence memory primarily encodes gist-level semantic content rather than surface syntactic form (Begg, 1971; Bregman & Strasberg, 1968).

3. **Wording recognition is robust but independent of noun memorability.** Participants could reliably detect voice mismatches (WR accuracy ≈ 0.73–0.76, well above chance), but this ability was unaffected by noun condition (*p* = .576). This dissociation — where noun memorability affects *whether* a sentence is recognised but not *how accurately* its surface form is judged — suggests that item recognition and form discrimination rely on partially independent memory representations.

## Limitations

The current analysis has several limitations that should be addressed:

- **Sample size.** The present sample of 112 participants is substantially below the pre-registered target of 334. Statistical power is therefore limited, particularly for detecting small effects and interactions. The marginally significant post-hoc comparisons (e.g., LL vs. LH, *p* = .077) may reach significance with a full sample.
- **Small effect sizes.** Even the significant noun condition effect was small (ε² = 0.013; Cliff's δ < 0.20). This limits the practical significance of the findings and underscores the need for the full planned sample.
- **Block effects.** The non-significant but numerically declining performance across blocks (Block 1 *M* = 0.699 → Block 3 *M* = 0.650) warrants monitoring with additional data, as proactive interference may become significant with more participants.
- **Lag confounds.** Although lag analysis showed only modest effects, the current quartile binning is coarse. A more fine-grained analysis or a mixed-effects model incorporating lag as a continuous predictor would better characterise the time-course of recognition.

## Future Directions

Several planned analyses remain for the full report:

1. **Complete data collection** to reach the pre-registered target of 334 participants, which will provide adequate power (≥ 0.80) to detect the small effects observed here.
2. **Mixed-effects modelling.** Generalised linear mixed-effects models (GLMMs) with crossed random effects for participants and items would more fully account for the nested data structure and allow simultaneous modelling of noun condition, voice, block, lag, and their interactions.
3. **Item-level analyses.** Examining whether specific sentences drive the condition-level effects (e.g., whether certain noun pairings are disproportionately memorable) would inform stimulus selection for future studies.
4. **Individual differences.** Exploring whether participant-level false alarm rates, overall hit rates, or response criteria moderate the noun condition effect could reveal distinct recognition strategies.
5. **Reaction time modelling.** Although no RT differences were found at the condition level, drift-diffusion or ex-Gaussian modelling of raw RT distributions could reveal speed–accuracy trade-offs masked by mean-level analyses.
6. **Semantic similarity controls.** Quantifying the semantic similarity between encoding and probe sentences (e.g., using word embedding distances) could help determine whether the LL disadvantage reflects weaker encoding, greater confusion with fillers, or both.

## Conclusion

The preliminary results support the hypothesis that noun imageability contributes to sentence memorability, but in a threshold rather than graded fashion. Grammatical voice does not modulate recognition or wording discrimination, consistent with gist-based models of sentence memory. These patterns are expected to strengthen and potentially reveal additional nuances once the full sample is collected and more sophisticated modelling techniques are applied.
