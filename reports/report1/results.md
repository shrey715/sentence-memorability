# Results

## Overview

A total of **112 participants** contributed data from **329 validated blocks** (97.9% pass rate; 7 of 336 possible participant-blocks failed the validation criterion). The mean false alarm rate across participants was *M* = 0.160 (range: 0.000–0.427). After correction (hit rate minus false alarm rate), the overall mean corrected recognition was *M* = 0.673 (Mdn = 0.708, range: −0.141 to 1.000).

Shapiro–Wilk tests confirmed that all four key metrics departed significantly from normality: IR corrected recognition (*W* = 0.96, *p* < .001), WR accuracy (*W* = 0.97, *p* < .001), IR RT (*W* = 0.97, *p* < .001), and WR RT (*W* = 0.85, *p* < .001). Non-parametric tests were therefore used throughout.

---

## 1. Effect of Noun Condition on Immediate Recognition (IR)

Per-participant corrected IR scores (collapsed across voice) by noun condition:

| Condition | *N* | *M* | *SD* | *Mdn* | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| HH | 112 | 0.689 | 0.185 | 0.740 | [0.655, 0.723] |
| HL | 112 | 0.691 | 0.177 | 0.708 | [0.658, 0.722] |
| LH | 112 | 0.679 | 0.188 | 0.729 | [0.645, 0.713] |
| LL | 112 | 0.632 | 0.189 | 0.656 | [0.596, 0.665] |

A Kruskal–Wallis test revealed a significant main effect of noun condition on corrected IR, *H*(3) = 11.26, *p* = .010, ε² = 0.013 (small effect). Post-hoc pairwise Wilcoxon tests (Holm-corrected) showed that LL was significantly lower than both HH (*p* = .024) and HL (*p* = .024). The LL vs. LH comparison approached significance (*p* = .077). All comparisons among HH, HL, and LH were non-significant (*p* = 1.00).

Cliff's delta effect sizes for pairwise comparisons confirmed small effects for all LL contrasts:

| Pair | δ | Magnitude | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|---|
| HH vs. LL | 0.188 | small | [0.037, 0.331] |
| HL vs. LL | 0.197 | small | [0.047, 0.339] |
| LH vs. LL | 0.164 | small | [0.014, 0.308] |
| HH vs. HL | 0.002 | negligible | [−0.149, 0.153] |
| HH vs. LH | 0.033 | negligible | [−0.119, 0.183] |
| HL vs. LH | 0.030 | negligible | [−0.120, 0.179] |

This pattern indicates a **threshold effect**: the presence of at least one high-memorability noun is sufficient to sustain recognition performance at a high level, with no additional benefit from having both nouns be high-memorability. Only when both nouns are low-memorability (LL) does corrected recognition decline significantly.

## 2. Effect of Noun Condition on Wording Recognition (WR)

| Condition | *N* | *M* | *SD* | *Mdn* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HH | 112 | 0.748 | 0.151 | 0.750 |
| HL | 112 | 0.747 | 0.145 | 0.750 |
| LH | 112 | 0.762 | 0.160 | 0.750 |
| LL | 112 | 0.730 | 0.173 | 0.750 |

A Kruskal–Wallis test found **no significant effect** of noun condition on WR accuracy, *H*(3) = 1.98, *p* = .576, ε² = 0.003. Noun memorability influences whether a sentence is recognised as familiar (IR), but does not affect how accurately participants discriminate the grammatical voice of the repeated sentence (WR).

## 3. Signal Detection Analysis

To complement the corrected recognition scores, signal detection theory indices were computed per condition:

| Condition | *M d'* | *SD d'* | *M c* | *SD c* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HH | 2.146 | 0.690 | −0.003 | 0.396 |
| HL | 2.146 | 0.654 | −0.003 | 0.407 |
| LH | 2.091 | 0.698 | 0.025 | 0.393 |
| LL | 1.925 | 0.672 | 0.107 | 0.421 |

A Kruskal–Wallis test on *d'* was significant, *H*(3) = 8.60, *p* = .035, mirroring the corrected IR result. Sensitivity was lowest in the LL condition. Response criterion *c* did not differ significantly across conditions, *H*(3) = 6.17, *p* = .104, indicating that the LL deficit reflects reduced discriminability rather than a shift in response bias.

## 4. Noun Condition × Voice Interaction

### Immediate Recognition

Mean corrected IR across the 8 condition × voice cells:

| Condition | Active *M* | Active *SD* | Passive *M* | Passive *SD* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HH | 0.683 | 0.199 | 0.695 | 0.220 |
| HL | 0.689 | 0.195 | 0.693 | 0.206 |
| LH | 0.677 | 0.202 | 0.681 | 0.216 |
| LL | 0.625 | 0.234 | 0.638 | 0.215 |

A Scheirer–Ray–Hare test showed a significant main effect of noun condition (*H* = 11.26, *p* = .010) but **no main effect of voice** (*H* = 0.76, *p* = .384) and **no noun condition × voice interaction** (*H* = 0.22, *p* = .975). The LL disadvantage is consistent across both active and passive voice.

### Wording Recognition

A Scheirer–Ray–Hare test on WR accuracy revealed no significant main effects of noun condition (*H* = 2.51, *p* = .473) or voice (*H* = 1.19, *p* = .276), and no interaction (*H* = 4.30, *p* = .231). WR accuracy was uniformly high across all 8 cells.

## 5. Voice Effects

### IR by Voice

| Voice | *N* | *M* | *SD* | *Mdn* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Active | 112 | 0.668 | 0.166 | 0.688 |
| Passive | 112 | 0.677 | 0.175 | 0.708 |

A paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test found **no significant voice effect** on corrected IR, *V* = 1704, *p* = .374. Whether a sentence was presented in active or passive voice did not influence subsequent recognition of its meaning.

### WR by Voice

| Voice | *N* | *M* | *SD* | *Mdn* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Active | 112 | 0.757 | 0.120 | 0.756 |
| Passive | 112 | 0.735 | 0.127 | 0.739 |

WR accuracy was numerically higher for active-voice sentences, but this difference was not statistically significant, *V* = 3279, *p* = .113. Critically, both voices were reliably above chance (0.5): Active *V* = 6322, *p* < .001; Passive *V* = 5990, *p* < .001. Participants could accurately detect voice mismatches regardless of the original grammatical form.

## 6. Reaction Time Analysis

### IR Reaction Time by Condition

| Condition | *N* | *M* (ms) | *SD* (ms) | *Mdn* (ms) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| HH | 112 | 1725 | 319 | 1618 |
| HL | 112 | 1729 | 365 | 1647 |
| LH | 112 | 1677 | 335 | 1589 |

A Kruskal–Wallis test found no significant effect of noun condition on IR RT, *H*(3) = 4.44, *p* = .218, ε² = 0.006. Despite the accuracy differences observed in corrected IR, participants did not take significantly longer to respond in any particular condition.

## 7. Block Effects

Mean corrected IR by block:

| Block | *N* | *M* | *SD* | *Mdn* |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 108 | 0.699 | 0.151 | 0.719 |
| 2 | 109 | 0.679 | 0.195 | 0.729 |
| 3 | 112 | 0.650 | 0.191 | 0.672 |

A Kruskal–Wallis test found **no significant block effect**, *H*(2) = 2.77, *p* = .250. The numerically declining trend (Block 1 > Block 2 > Block 3) is consistent with mild proactive interference accumulating across the session, but the effect does not reach statistical significance.

---

## 8. Lag Analysis

Trials were binned into quartiles by encoding-to-probe lag (number of intervening items):

| Lag Bin | *N* trials | Mean Lag | IR Hit Rate | WR Accuracy |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Q1 (short) | 1,601 | 5.7 | 0.847 | 0.754 |
| Q2 | 1,466 | 8.5 | 0.832 | 0.753 |
| Q3 | 1,371 | 10.4 | 0.815 | 0.766 |
| Q4 (long) | 826 | 12.8 | 0.837 | 0.744 |

IR hit rates were numerically highest at the shortest lags and showed a slight non-monotonic pattern, with a small recovery at the longest lags. WR accuracy remained stable across lag bins. These patterns suggest that encoding-to-probe distance has at most a modest influence on recognition in this paradigm.
